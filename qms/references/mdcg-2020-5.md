# MDCG 2020-5 - Clinical Evaluation — Equivalence: A Guide for Manufacturers and Notified Bodies

April 2020

This document has been endorsed by the Medical Device Coordination Group (MDCG) established by Article 103 of Regulation (EU) 2017/745. The MDCG is composed of representatives of all Member States and it is chaired by a representative of the European Commission. The document is not a European Commission document and it cannot be regarded as reflecting the official position of the European Commission. Any views expressed in this document are not legally binding and only the Court of Justice of the European Union can give binding interpretations of Union law.

## 1. Purpose

This guidance provides recommendations to manufacturers and notified bodies on how to demonstrate and assess equivalence in the context of clinical evaluation under the Medical Device Regulation (EU) 2017/745.

Under the MDR, manufacturers may use clinical data relating to an equivalent device to support the clinical evaluation of their device, subject to certain conditions. This document explains the requirements and provides practical guidance on the equivalence demonstration process.

## 2. Scope

This guidance applies to:

- All medical devices subject to the MDR (EU) 2017/745
- Clinical evaluation processes where the manufacturer intends to use equivalence
- Both new devices and devices being transitioned from the MDD to the MDR
- All risk classes, though the requirements are more stringent for Class III and implantable devices

## 3. Legal Framework

### 3.1 MDR Requirements for Equivalence

Article 61(5) of the MDR states that manufacturers may use clinical data relating to a device for which equivalence to the device in question can be demonstrated, subject to the following conditions:

1. The manufacturers have clearly demonstrated equivalence with respect to **technical**, **biological**, and **clinical** characteristics
2. There is a **contract** between the manufacturer and the manufacturer of the equivalent device giving explicit access to the technical documentation on an ongoing basis
3. The original clinical evaluation was performed in compliance with the requirements of the MDR

For Class III and implantable devices, the contract requirement (point 2) is mandatory. For other classes, it may be possible to demonstrate equivalence without such a contract if the equivalence is based on publicly available data.

### 3.2 Differences from MDD

The MDR introduces stricter requirements compared to the MDD:

- The requirement for a contract with the equivalent device manufacturer (Class III and implantable)
- More detailed criteria for demonstrating equivalence across all three characteristics
- Clearer requirements for justification of differences

## 4. Equivalence Characteristics

### 4.1 Technical Characteristics

The device should be of a similar design and used under similar conditions of use. Technical characteristics include:

- Design, including physical and chemical properties (dimensions, surface characteristics, energy source)
- Conditions of use (body site, contact duration, clinical environment)
- Similar specifications and properties (accuracy, sensitivity, specificity)
- Similar deployment methods
- Similar principles of operation

### 4.2 Biological Characteristics

The device should use the same materials or substances in contact with the same human tissues or body fluids. Biological characteristics include:

- Materials in contact with the body
- Substances in contact with the body
- Same duration and type of body contact
- Same release characteristics of substances (if applicable)

### 4.3 Clinical Characteristics

The device should be used for the same clinical condition, have the same intended purpose, at a similar site in the body, in a similar population. Clinical characteristics include:

- Same clinical condition or purpose
- Same intended purpose including the specific medical indications
- Similar site in the body
- Similar population (including age, anatomy, physiology)
- Same kind of user
- Similar performance under relevant clinical conditions

## 5. Demonstration of Equivalence

### 5.1 General Approach

The demonstration of equivalence should follow a structured approach:

1. **Identify the candidate equivalent device** — select a device that appears similar in technical, biological, and clinical characteristics
2. **Systematic comparison** — perform a detailed comparison across all three categories of characteristics
3. **Justify any differences** — for each identified difference, provide scientific justification that it does not affect the clinical performance or safety
4. **Document the assessment** — prepare a comprehensive equivalence report

### 5.2 Level of Evidence

The level of evidence required depends on:

- The risk class of the device
- The nature and extent of differences between the devices
- The availability and quality of clinical data for the equivalent device
- Whether direct access to the equivalent device's technical documentation is available

### 5.3 Limitations on Equivalence Claims

Equivalence cannot be claimed if:

- There are significant differences that could affect clinical performance or safety
- The manufacturer cannot adequately justify the identified differences
- There is insufficient clinical data available for the equivalent device
- The clinical conditions of use are materially different

## 6. Software-Specific Considerations

For medical device software (MDSW), equivalence assessment presents unique challenges:

### 6.1 Technical Equivalence for Software

- **Algorithm equivalence**: The software should use the same or similar algorithms, with comparable performance characteristics
- **Architecture equivalence**: Similar software architecture and data processing methods
- **Input/output equivalence**: Same types of inputs and outputs, with comparable specifications
- **Performance equivalence**: Similar processing capabilities and response times

### 6.2 Clinical Equivalence for Software

- Same intended clinical purpose and medical indications
- Same target patient population
- Same healthcare context (professional use, home use, etc.)
- Comparable clinical performance metrics

### 6.3 Challenges with AI/ML-Based Software

For AI/ML-based medical device software:

- Training data differences may affect clinical performance even if the algorithm architecture is similar
- The "black box" nature of some AI models makes technical comparison difficult
- Model performance may vary across different populations or clinical settings
- The rapidly evolving nature of AI means that equivalent devices may diverge over time

In general, demonstrating full equivalence for AI/ML-based software devices is challenging, and manufacturers are encouraged to generate their own clinical evidence through clinical investigations or well-designed performance studies.

## 7. Documentation Requirements

The equivalence assessment should be documented in the clinical evaluation report and include:

1. Identification of the equivalent device (name, manufacturer, version/model)
2. Description of both devices
3. Systematic comparison table covering all three characteristics
4. Justification for each identified difference
5. Conclusion on equivalence (full, partial, or none)
6. If partial equivalence: identification of gaps requiring additional clinical evidence
7. Contract or agreement (for Class III and implantable devices)

## 8. Role of Notified Bodies

Notified bodies should:

- Assess the adequacy of the equivalence demonstration
- Verify that all three categories of characteristics have been addressed
- Evaluate the scientific justification for any differences
- Confirm that the contract requirement is met (where applicable)
- Consider whether additional clinical evidence is needed to address any gaps
