# Literature Searches

Conduct a robust literature search to support clinical evaluation, risk management and state-of-the-art assessment.

A literature search is a systematic process for identifying, reviewing, and analysing published and non-published data relevant to a medical device. Like any scientific endeavour, the key is having a focused research question.

It is recommended that multiple literature searches using multiple databases are conducted to identify relevant literature to characterise the state-of-the-art, performance and safety data from similar devices.

## Minimum Requirements

These are the constituents of a literature search:

- **Search setup** - Provide details of the personnel who conducted the search, time period, databases used, search terms and record the number of studies identified.
- **Search protocol** - Provide details of the personnel who designed the protocol, the objective of the search and a summary of why it's necessary.
- **Research question** - Define the question guiding the search, formatted using PICO, SPIDER, or SPICE frameworks.
- **Search strategy** - Detail databases searched, search terms used and additional search methods (e.g. identifying grey literature).
- **Study selection process** - Screen abstracts against eligibility criteria, before performing a full-text review of potentially eligible studies and finally extracting data of studies chosen.
- **Data appraisal** - Assess how the literature contributes to the clinical evaluation and risk management file, based on methodological quality and relevance.

See the Deep dive section for more information on each of these elements, including examples of what to include.

## Common Pitfalls

As with any clinical evaluation activity, mistakes can be time-consuming and costly. So we want to help you avoid them! Here are some of the commonly pitfalls that we see.

> **Warning:** Issues that we encounter typically fall into one of three categories.
>
> **Search issues:**
>
> - Non-comprehensive search of scientific literature databases.
>   - A comprehensive search typically requires searching multiple databases.
>   - Database selection is influenced by the research question (e.g. Cochrane is best for high quality systematic reviews and meta-analyses).
> - Poorly structured search terms.
>   - Using NOT to exclude literature on children may lead to studies relevant to both children and adults being overlooked.
>   - Using AND with terms such as software or AI might overly limit the search to not include current standard practice.
> - A search that isn't recent.
>   - Justifying a search that is more than six months old may be difficult.
>
> **Insufficient detail:**
>
> Database and internet searches need to be documented with exact search terms and methods such that they can be reproduced. You can ensure the exact terms are included by copying your search directly from the database.
>
> **Lack of justification:**
>
> As there are many acceptable ways a literature search and review can be carried out, justification of decisions is important. MEDDEV 2.7/1 rev 4 includes the word(s) justif* 60 times!

## Desired Format

We really want to review your documents and these tips will enable us to do that:

- Upload narrative documents as text files (.DOCX or .PDF) formats for written reports.
- Upload spreadsheets (.XLSX) files for detailed or data-intensive evidence, like literature searches or appraisals of clinical data.
- Keep your files searchable, avoiding scanned PDFs or locked documents.

While not a requirement, we love to see a traceability matrix that connects clinical benefit claims to related clinical performance data. This has the following constituents:

- **Clinical benefit** - The claimed positive impact of a device on the health of an individual, patient management or public health.
- **Outcome measure** - The quantifiable metric used to assess the effectiveness, safety, or performance of a medical intervention.
- **Clinical performance claim** - A claimed ability of the device, resulting from its characteristics, to achieve its intended purpose, thereby leading to a clinical benefit.
- **State-of-the-art performance benchmark** - Related to some state-of-the-art data from similar devices, alternative pathways, or alternative treatments.
- **Clinical evaluation activity** - Clinical performance data generated by testing the device under evaluation or an equivalent device, in its intended target population and for its intended use.
- **Clinical evaluation activity endpoint** - The relevant clinical evaluation variable that was measured to determine the effect of the intervention.
- **Clinical performance data** - Clinical performance findings from the clinical evaluation activity.

## Deep Dive

### Expected Content: Search Setup

Provide the following details:

- **Search authors** - Provide details of the personnel responsible for executing the search.
- **Data source** - Specify the databases used for the search.
- **Search terms** - List the search terms and full search logic used.
- **Search period** - Define the start and end dates of the literature search period.
- **Date of execution** - State the date the literature search was conducted.
- **Studies identified** - Record the number of studies found through the database search, prior and following the application of inclusion/exclusion criteria.

#### Search Terms

Performing a literature search to determine the clinical standard of care for diagnosing melanoma, three core concepts can be extracted. The addition of boolean logic and filters can help narrow the search to identify the most relevant studies while minimising retrieval of irrelevant ones.

*Concept 1 - Condition:*
- melanoma OR "malignant melanoma" OR "cutaneous melanoma" OR "skin cancer" OR "pigmented lesion\*" OR "melanocytic lesion\*"

*Concept 2 - Diagnosis:*
- diagnos\* OR detection OR screening OR "early detection" OR identification OR assessment OR evaluation OR examination

*Concept 3 - Standard of care:*
- "standard of care" OR "clinical practice" OR "practice guideline\*" OR "clinical guideline\*" OR protocol\* OR pathway\* OR algorithm\* OR "diagnostic pathway\*" OR "clinical pathway\*" OR "best practice\*" OR dermoscopy OR dermatoscopy OR biopsy OR histopatholog\* OR "visual examination" OR "clinical examination"

### Expected Content: Literature Search Protocol

Provide the following details:

- **Protocol authors** - List the personnel responsible for designing the literature search protocol.
- **Purpose** - Clearly state the purpose of the literature search. Choose from standard options (e.g., support an equivalence claim, assess state of the art).
- **Background** - Provide a concise summary describing why this search is necessary and how it complements previous searches or addresses data gaps.
- **Equivalence** - If applicable, provide details of the equivalence claim this search supports.

### Expected Content: Research Question

For a medical device that analyses skin images and uses a convolutional neural network to improve melanoma detection compared with standard visual inspection, an appropriate search question would be:

*"What is the current clinical standard of care for diagnosing melanoma in patients with suspicious skin lesions?"*

Typically the research question is formatted using a standard framework, such as PICO, SPICE or SPIDER.

**P - Population/Patient:** Adults presenting with suspicious pigmented skin lesions or undergoing skin cancer screening in primary care, dermatology, or specialist skin cancer clinic settings.

**I - Intervention/Index Test:** Current clinical standard of care diagnostic methods for melanoma, including:

- Clinical visual examination.
- Dermoscopy/dermatoscopy.
- Excisional or incisional biopsy followed by histopathological examination.
- Sequential digital dermoscopy imaging (where applicable).
- Established diagnostic algorithms and referral pathways.

**C - Comparator:** Not applicable for this literature search (the purpose is to establish what the current standard is, rather than to compare interventions).

> For future comparative effectiveness studies, the comparator would be the smartphone-based CNN SaMD device versus current standard methods.

**O - Outcome:**

- Diagnostic accuracy metrics (sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, negative predictive value).
- Current clinical practice pathways and protocols.
- Established performance benchmarks.
- Number needed to biopsy/excise ratios.

Once defined, explain why the research question is appropriate for the clinical evaluation. It is important to describe how the literature search informs or supports risk management.

With a clearly defined research question, the following criteria can be applied to the search.

**Inclusion Criteria:**

- Studies involving adult populations.
- Studies describing melanoma diagnostic protocols and pathways.
- Clinical practice guidelines for melanoma detection and diagnosis.
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of melanoma diagnostic methods.
- Studies reporting diagnostic accuracy of current standard methods (dermoscopy, visual examination, biopsy).
- Consensus statements from dermatology societies.
- Publication date: 2015-present (to capture current practice standards).

**Exclusion Criteria:**

- Studies focused solely on melanoma treatment rather than diagnosis.
- Studies of experimental or investigational diagnostic methods not yet part of standard care.
- Case reports or case series with fewer than 10 patients.
- Studies focused exclusively on melanoma subtypes not relevant to cutaneous melanoma (e.g., ocular melanoma).

### Expected Content: Literature Search Strategy

Continuing with the example of our melanoma detection device, having specified the research question and applied appropriate constraints, the next step is to detail the search strategy used.

For the database search, we've gone for all of the big hitters. Multiple databases are typically chosen to achieve a comprehensive search. As for grey literature sources, these will depend on the clinical condition in question. A few examples of relevant guidelines for melanoma have been provided.

List the databases searched (examples below) and justify each selection e.g. Cochrane provides high-quality systemic reviews to help determine diagnostic accuracy. Similarly, justify your approach to finding and including non-published data (examples of grey literature provided below).

**Databases searched:**

- PubMed/MEDLINE
- Embase
- Cochrane Library
- Web of Science
- Scopus

**Grey Literature Sources:**

- American Academy of Dermatology (AAD) guidelines
- European Society for Medical Oncology (ESMO) guidelines
- National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines
- Skin Cancer Foundation resources
- Review of FDA 510(k) databases for predicate devices and their performance benchmarks

### Expected Content: Study Selection

There are two elements to study selection:

1. **Title and abstract screening:** Review titles and abstracts to quickly assess whether the studies are relevant.
2. **Full-text screening:** For studies that pass the initial review, examine the full text to determine their suitability based on the predefined criteria.

For published literature reviews, there is often more than one reviewer who screens and reviews articles for eligibility. In this circumstance, it is necessary to state how disagreements were resolved and determine the reliability of selection using a statistical test such as Cohen's kappa. For the purposes of a technical file, one reviewer should suffice.

Two key things to document are reasons for exclusion of a study and where these results will be managed, for example using systematic review software such as Covidence. It is also worth outlining how you will identify and manage duplicate records.

### Expected Content: Data Appraisal

#### Appraisal Process

- **Access to full text** - Confirm whether the full text was available for each document.
- **Appraisal results** - Assess each piece of literature against predefined criteria.
- **Limitations and errors** - Identify any methodological limitations or errors and assess their impact on data integrity.
- **Conflicts of interest** - Report any potential conflicts in the literature and assess their significance.
- **Clinical investigation claims** - State whether the clinical investigation design is sufficient to support performance or safety claims.
- **Validity of conclusions** - Assess whether conclusions in the literature are supported by the data.

#### Quality Assessment

Once the relevant studies are selected, a critical appraisal assesses the quality, validity, and reliability of the findings.

There are two key domains to appraise:

1. Methodological quality and scientific validity.
2. Relevance to the clinical evaluation.

Methodological quality and scientific validity considerations include aspects such as confounding influences, bias, adequacy of the sample size and endpoints, blinding of users or assessors, and adequacy of the follow up period.

Considerations relevant to the clinical evaluation are if device or pathway shares the same intended purpose and the representativeness of the target population. Whether the use environment is comparable to that of your device is also a factor to consider.

There are many established tools and resources for critical appraisal including, but not limited to:

- Cochrane Methods Network
- GRADE (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluations)
- International Medical Device Regulators Forum Clinical evaluation resources

#### Data Extraction

Returning to the example of our melanoma detection device, for each study included, the following information should be extracted.

**Study characteristics:**

- Author, year, country, study design.
- Setting (primary care, specialist dermatology, etc.).
- Sample size and population characteristics.

**Diagnostic pathway information:**

- Initial assessment methods (visual examination, patient history).
- Use of dermoscopy (frequency, who performs it).
- Criteria for biopsy referral.
- Role of histopathological examination.
- Diagnostic algorithms or scoring systems used (e.g. ABCDE criteria).

**Performance benchmarks:**

- Sensitivity and specificity of current methods.
- Positive and negative predictive values.
- Number needed to biopsy ratios.
- Diagnostic accuracy of dermatologists vs. general practitioners.

#### Analysis

Finally, you need to synthesise the findings and report the results of the literature review. This should be done in relation to the original research question, but also include safety and performance benchmarks.

Describe how findings or data derived from the appraised literature provide evidence for the clinical performance and safety of:

- The device under evaluation.
- Similar devices.
- Alternative treatments.
- Alternative pathways.
