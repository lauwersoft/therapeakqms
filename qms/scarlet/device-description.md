# Device Description

Craft a device description that features essential regulatory information and is compliant with EU MDR.

The device description provides a complete and structured profile of the medical device, including its technical characteristics, classification, regulatory identifiers, and supporting documentation. It supports classification decisions, conformity assessment, and device registration requirements under EU MDR.

## Minimum Requirements

Include the following elements in your device description:

- **Device identification** - Product or trade name, general description, and unique device identifier (UDI).
- **Function** - Intended purpose and indications, principles of operation, novel features, and key functional elements.
- **Classification** - Rationale for qualification as a medical device and risk class with justification.
- **Device variants, accessories and history** - Accessories, device configurations and variations, and references to previous and similar generations.
- **Technical specifications** - Raw materials (where applicable) and technical specifications including features and performance attributes.

A full list of expected content is provided in the Deep dive section.

## Common Pitfalls

Naturally, questions crop up around device classification, specifically the prospect of applying for class I certification. Sorry to disappoint but class I is rarely the right fit...

> **Warning:** We understand that having a Class I medical device can be appealing, offering a simpler route to market. Unfortunately the statements below are inadequate justification for Class I designation.
>
> **"Devices similar to ours are Class I"**
>
> The developer of an incorrectly classified device takes on a lot of risk hoping a competent authority doesn't take a closer look at their device and the legitimacy of the classification decision.
>
> However, to continue the thought experiment, let's say you are convinced that their medical device is Class I and the device seems similar to yours. The type of documentation required in order to draw accurate comparisons between one device and another is often not readily available.
>
> **"We're going to release a Class I device first, collect clinical data then go for Class IIa"**
>
> Demonstrating 'clinical performance' is a key aspect of the clinical evaluation process. Clinical performance is the ability of a device to achieve a clinical benefit when used as intended.
>
> So, in order to generate the necessary clinical data to support a Class IIa certification, the Class I device would need to be used outside the scope of its intended purpose.
>
> **"My device is Class I in the UK, so it can be Class I in the EU"**
>
> The EU and UK have different regulations, and they classify medical devices, including software, differently.
>
> When classifying devices in multiple jurisdictions, it's best to review the respective classification rules and associated guidance and identify the appropriate classification for each jurisdiction. This is something that we're more than happy to help with!

### Risk Classification

The classification of your device has implications on the regulatory expectations when it comes to documentation and evidence needed to substantiate performance claims.

Rule 11 should provide clarity if there's uncertainty:

> *Software intended to provide information which is used to take decisions with diagnosis or therapeutic purposes is classified as class IIa, except if such decisions have an impact that may cause:*
>
> *- death or an irreversible deterioration of a person's state of health, in which case it is in class III; or*
>
> *- a serious deterioration of a person's state of health or a surgical intervention, in which case it is classified as class IIb.*
>
> *Software intended to monitor physiological processes is classified as class IIa, except if it is intended for monitoring of vital physiological parameters, where the nature of variations of those parameters is such that it could result in immediate danger to the patient, in which case it is classified as class IIb.*
>
> *All other software is classified as class I.*

If you're still unsure, please reach out to us. We'll quickly help to determine which classification is appropriate.

### Expected Content: Device Identification

**The product or trade name and a general description**

The description should include the intended purpose of the device and its intended users.

**Unique Device Identification (UDI)**

If your device does not yet have a UDI, you should provide clear identification by means of product code, catalogue number or other unambiguous reference that allows traceability.

### Expected Content: Function

**The intended purpose and indications of the device**

This information should include the intended patient population and medical conditions to be diagnosed, treated and/or monitored and other considerations such as patient selection criteria, indications, contra-indications and warnings.

**A description of the principles of operation**

Describe the principles of operation of the device and its mode of action. This encompasses how users interact with the device and how it achieves its intended purpose.

**An explanation of novel features**

Explain any features of the device that are novel compared with other devices on the market and or/novel compared with other devices from the same manufacturer.

**A general description of key functional elements**

A description of the device's parts/components (including software), its functionality and, where applicable, its qualitative and quantitative composition. Where appropriate, this should include labelled diagrams including sufficient explanation to understand the diagrams.

### Expected Content: Classification

**A rationale for the qualification of the product as a device**

Explain how the product qualifies as a medical device. See the Rationale for qualification as a medical device section for more information.

**The risk class of the device**

Include the risk class and justification for the classification. See the Risk classification section for more information.

### Expected Content: Device Variants, Accessories and History

**A description of accessories for the device**

Describe other devices, accessories and/or other products that are intended to be used in conjunction with the device under assessment.

**A description of device configurations and/or variations**

Describe or provide a complete list of any configurations or variations of the device that are intended to be made available on the market.

**Reference to previous and similar generations of the device**

In addition to the above information about the current device, you should include information regarding:

- Previous generations of the device produced by the manufacturer, if applicable.
- An overview of devices that have been identified as similar that are available on the EU or international markets if applicable.

### Expected Content: Technical Specifications

**A description of the raw materials incorporated into key functional elements**

This is only applicable if your device includes hardware, where the hardware is integral to the device and not an accessory.

**A description of technical specifications**

Describe technical specifications such as features and performance attributes of the device (and any variations/configurations of it) and accessories that would typically appear in the product specification made available to the user (e.g. in brochures and catalogues).
