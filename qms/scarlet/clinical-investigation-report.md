# Clinical Investigation Report

Create a clinical investigation report that provides information to demonstrate the safety and performance of your investigational device.

The clinical investigation report (CIR) documents the results and conclusions of a clinical investigation conducted on a medical device. It is a core part of the clinical evidence submitted for EU MDR conformity, offering a summary of study design, execution, and outcomes.

The sponsor of a clinical investigation shall submit a report of the clinical investigation within one year of the end of the clinical investigation or within three months of the early termination.

## Minimum Requirements

The following elements constitute a robust CIR:

- **Investigation details** - Include the single identification number, registration number, a reference to the clinical investigation plan, start date, completion date, study sites, funding details and details of ethical approval.
- **Personnel involved** - Specify the sponsor details, details of the coordinating investigator and information pertaining to any other personnel or organisations involved (e.g. clinicians, biostatisticians, contract research organisations, subcontractors).
- **Context** - Provide a brief introduction which places the clinical investigation in the context of the development of the investigational device.
- **Investigation summary** - Outline the purpose of the investigation, with the hypotheses addressed and corresponding endpoints, along with details of the study design.
- **Conclusion** - Discuss the clinical relevance of the findings and the limitations of the investigation.

## Common Pitfalls

It can be tempting to draw conclusions that embellish the safety and performance of your device. Below are pitfalls that we encounter pertaining to the evaluation of study findings.

> **Warning:** These pitfalls are explained using the example of a comparative effectiveness study whose objective is to demonstrate superiority of a SaMD in detecting melanoma compared to standard care.
>
> **Confusing statistical significance with clinical significance:** Assuming that a statistically significant improvement in sensitivity (e.g. p=0.03), demonstrates clinical benefit.
>
> - A p-value only tells you the result is unlikely due to chance, not whether it matters clinically. A 1% improvement in sensitivity might be statistically significant but clinically meaningless.
>
> **Ignoring the intended use context**: Discussing performance metrics (e.g. 92% sensitivity) in isolation without relating them to where/how the device will actually be used.
>
> - 92% sensitivity sounds good, but is this for: primary care screening, dermatology specialist consultation or teledermatology triage? Each context has different clinical requirements and consequences.
>
> **Cherry-picking favourable metrics**: Emphasising sensitivity (92%) while downplaying specificity (65%).
>
> - 65% specificity means 35% of benign lesions flagged as suspicious. This could lead to unnecessary biopsies, patient anxiety, and healthcare costs.

## Desired Format

We really want to review your documents and these tips will enable us to do that:

- Upload narrative documents as text files (.DOCX or .PDF) formats for written reports.
- Upload spreadsheets (.XLSX) files for detailed or data-intensive evidence, like literature searches or appraisals of clinical data.
- Keep your files searchable, avoiding scanned PDFs or locked documents.

While not a requirement, we love to see a traceability matrix that connects clinical benefit claims to related clinical performance data. This has the following constituents:

- **Clinical benefit** - The claimed positive impact of a device on the health of an individual, patient management or public health.
- **Outcome measure** - The quantifiable metric used to assess the effectiveness, safety, or performance of a medical intervention.
- **Clinical performance claim** - A claimed ability of the device, resulting from its characteristics, to achieve its intended purpose, thereby leading to a clinical benefit.
- **State-of-the-art performance benchmark** - Related to some state-of-the-art data from similar devices, alternative pathways, or alternative treatments.
- **Clinical evaluation activity** - Clinical performance data generated by testing the device under evaluation or an equivalent device, in its intended target population and for its intended use.
- **Clinical evaluation activity endpoint** - The relevant clinical evaluation variable that was measured to determine the effect of the intervention.
- **Clinical performance data** - Clinical performance findings from the clinical evaluation activity.

## Deep Dive

### Expected Content: Investigation Summary

A lot of the information below will be obtained from the *Clinical investigation plan.* It's important to highlight any deviations from the investigation plan and justify these.

- Specify the purpose of the investigation, study design and hypotheses addressed.
- State the investigation objectives and endpoints, along with its duration.
- Outline the procedures and methods for how the device was used during the investigation.
- Describe the eligibility criteria for participants and, where applicable, for the centres involved for the investigation.
- Detail the subject population (sample size and clinical population details), as well as the number of participants screened, enrolled, allocated, withdrawn, and followed-up.
- Explain the methods for randomisation and blinding, if applicable.
- Detail statistical analysis methods: such as to compare groups, assess primary and secondary outcomes, hypotheses testing, deal with missing data, and adjust for biases.
- Describe key results: baseline demographics, sample clinical characteristics, performance, safety outcomes, adverse events, and device deficiencies.
- State any deviations from the clinical investigation plan.

### Expected Content: Analysis and Conclusion

This is where we find out if your study substantiates the device's clinical benefit claims.

The discussion should evaluate clinical performance and/or safety, feeding into an assessment of benefits and risks. These findings will contribute to both your *Clinical evaluation report* and *Risk management report*.

The investigation carried out may constitute part of your post-market activities, in which case the *Post-market clinical follow-up plan* should be referenced.

It's important to highlight any limitations of this study and gaps in evidence. Explain how the study fits into your evidence generation roadmap, detailing any plans for future studies.
