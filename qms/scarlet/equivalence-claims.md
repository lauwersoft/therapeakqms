# Equivalence Claims

Learn whether an equivalence is viable as part of your evidence generation efforts.

Equivalence may be used to justify reliance on existing clinical data for the equivalent device to support safety and performance claims. It includes three parts: the identification of the equivalent device, a formal equivalence claim, and a comparative analysis of technical and clinical characteristics.

> **Info:** Regulatory scrutiny is strict, particularly regarding algorithm transparency, cybersecurity risks, and data access. Speak to a member of the team to see whether claiming equivalence is viable.

## Minimum Requirements

Below is an outline of what we expect to see in an equivalence claim document:

- **Equivalent device** - Describe the medical device to which equivalence is being claimed (name, version, manufacturer's trade name), including a reference to the literature search protocol used to identify the device or support the equivalence claim.
- **Safety and performance claims** - Provide a summary of safety and performance claims of the equivalent device, along with supporting evidence. Indicate whether the equivalent device represents the clinical state of the art.
- **Equivalence claim** - Compare the subject device and the proposed equivalent device in both technical and clinical terms.
- **Conclusion** - Indicate whether the equivalence claim is valid based on your analyses. Provide a justification summarising how and why the devices are deemed equivalent.

## Common Pitfalls

The most common pitfall is **making an equivalence claim without having access to the necessary information** needed to demonstrate equivalence.

> **Warning:** Manufacturers are not inclined to share their secret sauce and without access to comprehensive documentation that includes technical specifications and algorithm details, equivalence cannot be justified.

## Desired Format

We really want to review your documents and these tips will enable us to do that:

- Upload narrative documents as text files (.DOCX or .PDF) formats for written reports.
- Upload spreadsheets (.XLSX) files for detailed or data-intensive evidence, like literature searches or appraisals of clinical data.
- Keep your files searchable, avoiding scanned PDFs or locked documents.

While not a requirement, we love to see a traceability matrix that connects clinical benefit claims to related clinical performance data. This has the following constituents:

- **Clinical benefit** - The claimed positive impact of a device on the health of an individual, patient management or public health.
- **Outcome measure** - The quantifiable metric used to assess the effectiveness, safety, or performance of a medical intervention.
- **Clinical performance claim** - A claimed ability of the device, resulting from its characteristics, to achieve its intended purpose, thereby leading to a clinical benefit.
- **State-of-the-art performance benchmark** - Related to some state-of-the-art data from similar devices, alternative pathways, or alternative treatments.
- **Clinical evaluation activity** - Clinical performance data generated by testing the device under evaluation or an equivalent device, in its intended target population and for its intended use.
- **Clinical evaluation activity endpoint** - The relevant clinical evaluation variable that was measured to determine the effect of the intervention.
- **Clinical performance data** - Clinical performance findings from the clinical evaluation activity.

## Deep Dive

### Comparative Analysis

Support the equivalence claim using a set of comparative evidence and analysis for both technical and clinical characteristics - equivalence claims aren't taken lightly!

**Same and similar characteristic analysis**

This section involves a deep comparison of specific characteristics between the device under evaluation and the equivalent device.

- Clearly identify the characteristic being analysed (e.g., design, software, deployment method).
- Describe the characteristic as it relates to both the subject device and the equivalent device.
- Indicate the type of data used (e.g., technical documentation, literature).
- Provide details (e.g. links) to any relevant literature or clinical data appraisal.
- List the differences observed between the devices.
- Evaluate whether these differences result in any clinically significant differences in clinical performance or safety. Include justification for your decision.

**Technical characteristics**

Use comparative evidence and analysis for:

- Critical technical performance requirements
- Design
- Conditions of use in the EU and UK
- Specifications
- Software algorithms
- Deployment methods
- Principles of operation

**Clinical characteristics**

Use comparative evidence and analysis for:

- Intended purpose.
- Intended patient population.
- Clinical conditions and indications.
- Users.
- Critical clinical performance requirements.

For EU and UK submissions:

- Intended purpose, clinical indications, and users must be the same.
- Intended patient population and performance requirements must be similar, with no clinically significant differences.

Scientific justification is needed to justify why there would be no clinically significant difference in the safety and clinical performance of the device.

### Worked Example

Let's explore how to approach an equivalence claim with a worked example.

Company 1 has a CE-marked SaMD that predicts stroke risk based on blood pressure data. Company 2 has developed a device that predicts stroke risk based on blood pressure and cholesterol-levels prediction, and has a similar intended purpose, clinical condition, user profile, and patient population.

**Device under evaluation: Company 2**

- Intended purpose: Integrates with an EHR using blood pressure and lipid count to provide clinicians with an estimation of total cholesterol and stroke risk over 5 years in adults aged 45-80 years.
- Clinical performance: 92% accuracy, sensitivity/specificity balanced.
- Technical performance: Real-time prediction with cloud sync.

**Proposed equivalent device: Company 1**

- Integrates with an EHR using blood pressure to provide clinicians with an estimation of stroke risk over 5 years in adults aged 30-75 years.
- Clinical performance: 91% accuracy, similar balance.
- Technical performance: Real-time prediction.

**Evaluation**

- As the devices have different manufacturers, access to key documentation such as instructions for use, software algorithm descriptions, validation studies, and risk management is required (likely through a data-sharing agreement).
- Although the algorithm has additional data input and outputs, the principle of operation and clinical performance remain consistent.
- A gap analysis can be conducted to assess the impact of the new input parameter (cholesterol) and demonstrate no clinically significant differences.

**Conclusion**

A valid equivalence claim is likely possible, leveraging existing clinical data and supporting documentation, provided any new performance characteristics are addressed with scientific justification.
