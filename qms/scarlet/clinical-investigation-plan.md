# Clinical Investigation Plan

Design a clinical investigation to generate robust, ethical, and regulatory-compliant evidence.

The Clinical Investigation Plan (CIP) defines the rationale, objectives, methodology, and administrative framework of a clinical investigation. It is a core document supporting the conformity assessment procedure, particularly where clinical investigations are required to demonstrate compliance with EU MDR Annex XIV and Annex XV.

## Minimum Requirements

A solid CIP comprises the following:

- **Administrative details** - Specify the date the CIP was finalised and assign a unique identifier. Describe investigation sites, patient population, funding sources and the involvement of any external organisations.
- **Ethics review and informed consent** - Ensure that all participating sites have relevant ethical clearance and describe how informed consent will be obtained from subjects before participation.
- **Investigational protocol** - The protocol should cover objectives, design, methodology and statistical considerations. For SaMD, this includes specifications of the software version, intended use environment, hardware/operating system requirements, and data management procedures.
- **Statistical design and analysis** - Specify the population being analysed and outline the statistical methods used to analyse data. These include sample size calculation, handling of missing data, subgroup analysis (if applicable) and bias control methods. Describe methods to reduce confounding (e.g. stratification, regression adjustment or propensity scoring).
- **Investigator's brochure** - Include the rationale for the clinical investigation, evidence of clinical safety and performance, along with essential instructions for the safe use of the device.

## Common Pitfalls

The common pitfalls that we encounter are often to do with statistical design and analysis, go figure.

> **Warning:** It pains us when **sample size has not been calculated** or when this **process is reverse-engineered** so that the calculation is based on what is convenient, rather than what is required for each endpoint. When a sample size is reported, we expect to see the assumptions that underpin it. This includes justification for non-inferiority or equivalence margins.
>
> Another common pitfall is **ignoring attrition**, leading to underpowered analyses when fewer cases are evaluable than planned. An analysis may also be underpowered if the disease prevalence is treated as fixed when diagnostic performance varies across populations.

## Desired Format

We really want to review your documents and these tips will enable us to do that:

- Upload narrative documents as text files (.DOCX or .PDF) formats for written reports.
- Upload spreadsheets (.XLSX) files for detailed or data-intensive evidence, like literature searches or appraisals of clinical data.
- Keep your files searchable, avoiding scanned PDFs or locked documents.

While not a requirement, we love to see a traceability matrix that connects clinical benefit claims to related clinical performance data. This has the following constituents:

- **Clinical benefit** - The claimed positive impact of a device on the health of an individual, patient management or public health.
- **Outcome measure** - The quantifiable metric used to assess the effectiveness, safety, or performance of a medical intervention.
- **Clinical performance claim** - A claimed ability of the device, resulting from its characteristics, to achieve its intended purpose, thereby leading to a clinical benefit.
- **State-of-the-art performance benchmark** - Related to some state-of-the-art data from similar devices, alternative pathways, or alternative treatments.
- **Clinical evaluation activity** - Clinical performance data generated by testing the device under evaluation or an equivalent device, in its intended target population and for its intended use.
- **Clinical evaluation activity endpoint** - The relevant clinical evaluation variable that was measured to determine the effect of the intervention.
- **Clinical performance data** - Clinical performance findings from the clinical evaluation activity.

## Deep Dive

### Expected Content: Administrative Details

**Basic information**

- **Title**: Include the full title of the clinical investigation in a way that reflects its scope and population.
- **Date**: Specify the date the CIP was finalised.
- **Identification number**: Assign a unique identifier to the clinical investigation, consistent with internal tracking or EUDAMED identifiers where relevant.

**Patient population**

Ensure the population is representative of the target population for the device's intended purpose, as required under MDR Annex XV and ISO 14155.

Describe the participant population:

- Type of cohort (e.g. intervention, comparator, control).
- Inclusion and exclusion criteria.
- Estimated number of subjects.

**Sponsor details and sites**

Include the name, address and contact details for the organisation responsible for initiation and management of a clinical investigation.

Similarly, describe each site where the investigation will take place. The following information is typically expected:

- Name and address of the site.
- Principal investigator details.
- Details of other investigators at the site (as applicable).

It is also expected that funding sources and details of insurance coverage are included.

**Financing and insurance**

- Describe funding sources and financial arrangements.
- Outline subject insurance coverage in accordance with national and EU legal requirements (see EU MDR Annex XV, Chapter II, Section 4.3).

### Expected Content: Ethics Review and Informed Consent

The following details should typically be documented to demonstrate appropriate ethics oversight:

- The full name of the ethics committee responsible for the review.
- The geographic area of jurisdiction for the ethics committee (e.g. UK, EU).
- The date on which the ethics committee completed its review.
- Whether the activity under review was approved by the ethics committee.
- Summarise any conditions, findings, or limitations identified by the ethics committee as part of its review.

Describe how informed consent will be obtained from subjects before participation. Include procedures for vulnerable populations (e.g. minors, incapacitated individuals, emergency situations), compensation if applicable, and documentation methods.

### Expected Content: Investigational Protocol

**Device description**

Provide a description of the investigational device, including:

- Intended purpose in the investigation
- Device model/type and version
- Summary of materials in contact with tissues or body fluids (if applicable)
- Technical and functional features
- Description of how traceability is ensured (e.g. batch or serial numbers)
- Describe how access to the investigational device will be controlled, and how distribution, use, return, or disposal will be documented.

**Objectives, endpoints and hypotheses**

As with any form of research activity, start by defining the objectives of the investigation. These can be derived from the device's clinical benefit claims. The study objectives form the basis of the study endpoints, from which you can generate a hypothesis.

The primary endpoint directly addresses the study objective, there should usually be only one primary endpoint (or a composite endpoint) in confirmatory studies.

Every primary endpoint has a corresponding hypothesis. This should have directionality, making clear whether the study is testing superiority, non-inferiority, or equivalence.

Returning to the example of our melanoma detection device, the proposed **clinical benefit claim** is:

> *Device X improves early melanoma detection compared to standard visual inspection.*

From this we can specify the **study objective**:

> *To determine if Device X has a higher sensitivity for malignant lesions compared with standard practice.*

For this study, appropriate **endpoints** include:

> *Primary endpoint: Sensitivity of Device X for histologically confirmed melanoma at initial consultation.*
> *Secondary endpoint: Time to diagnosis.*

Finally we arrive at the **study hypothesis**:

> *H0: Sensitivity(Device X) <= Sensitivity(visual inspection).*
> *H1: Sensitivity(Device X) > Sensitivity(visual inspection).*

**Study design**

Here is an outline of a study which aims to test the above hypothesis. This is a prospective, multi-center, single-arm, paired diagnostic accuracy study with blinded evaluations.

- **Investigation cohort**: Adult patients presenting to dermatology clinics with suspicious pigmented skin lesions requiring clinical evaluation for possible melanoma.
- **Inclusion criteria**:
  1. Age: >=18 years at time of enrolment
  2. Lesion Characteristics:
     - Presence of at least one pigmented skin lesion suspicious for melanoma or requiring evaluation as determined by the treating dermatologist
     - Lesion(s) fall into one of two categories:
       - Category A (Biopsied): Deemed to require biopsy or excision based on clinical judgment per standard practice, OR
       - Category B (Non-biopsied): Not deemed to require immediate biopsy but requiring monitoring/surveillance per standard practice
  3. Evaluability:
     - Lesion(s) must be accessible for imaging by Device X
     - For Category A: Lesion(s) must be amenable to complete excision or biopsy with sufficient tissue for histopathological diagnosis
     - For Category B: Lesion(s) must be clearly documented and photographable for tracking during follow-up
  4. Consent: Ability to provide written informed consent.
- **Exclusion criteria**:
  1. Prior History:
     - Known diagnosis of melanoma at the target lesion site prior to enrollment
     - Previous treatment (excision, radiation, topical therapy) of the target lesion
  2. Medical Conditions:
     - For Category A: Conditions that preclude safe biopsy or excision (e.g., severe bleeding disorders, anticoagulation that cannot be temporarily held per institutional protocols)
     - Life expectancy <12 months (precludes adequate follow-up for Category B lesions)
     - Medical instability requiring immediate treatment that would delay diagnostic procedures
  3. Technical Limitations:
     - Inability to remain still for Device X imaging acquisition
     - Skin conditions that interfere with image quality (severe inflammation, ulceration covering the lesion)
  4. Previously biopsied or treated lesions at the target site
  5. For Category B lesions: Lesions that cannot be reliably identified or tracked over 12-month follow-up period
- **Investigation sites**: Multi-center study conducted at 3-5 dermatology centres.
- **Paired design**: Each patient evaluated by both standard care and SaMD.
- **Blinded evaluation**: Dermatologists blinded to SaMD results during standard care assessment.
- **Sequential assessment**: Standard care -> SaMD -> Reference standard.
- **Reference standard**: Histopathology for biopsied lesions; 12-month clinical follow-up for non-biopsied lesions.
- **Total study duration**: 24 months.
- **Enrolment period**: 18 months.
- **Follow-up period**: 12 months from last patient enrolled.
- **Per-patient participation**: Up to 12 months (if not biopsied).

### Core Statistical Concepts

**Sample size and power**

Clinical studies must form a coherent chain from claims -> objectives -> endpoints -> hypotheses. This chain is only meaningful if the study is large enough (and designed appropriately) to deliver evidence that is both statistically and clinically credible.

The sample size for our study that looks to determine if Device X has higher sensitivity for malignant lesions compared with standard practice is governed by how many cases are needed to detect the expected effect in sensitivity. This must take into account melanoma prevalence, data attrition, and confidence interval width.

As a general rule, three main parameters will be needed to compute the sample size requirements:

- **Type 1 error**
  - This is the probability of a false positive and is typically set at 5%.
  - Type 1 error must be controlled at the study level, not just per endpoint so if the study has multiple hypotheses, the overall error must still be <=5%.
    - One way to control for multiplicity is to divide the significance level (e.g. 0.05) across the number of tests (known as the Bonferroni correction).
- **Power**
  - This is the probability of detecting the effect if it truly exists and is typically 80-90% in medical studies.
  - If multiple co-primary endpoints must all succeed, each endpoint needs more than 80-90% power individually to maintain overall power.
- **Effect size**
  - This is the difference between device and comparator or value of reference (for superiority) or the margin chosen (for NI/equivalence).
  - Should reflect what is clinically meaningful, not what is easiest to demonstrate: claiming Device X is superior if sensitivity improves by 2% may be statistically feasible but clinically irrelevant; a 10% difference may be the appropriate effect size to power for.

**Statistical analysis plan**

The statistical analysis plan sets out:

- Which populations will be analysed.
- Which statistical methods will be used.
- How missing data will be handled.
- How multiplicity and sensitivity analyses will be managed.

Analyses must align with the hypotheses that flow from the objectives and endpoints. If the objective is to demonstrate non-inferiority, then the analysis must present confidence intervals relative to the pre-specified margin. If the objective is to show superiority, the analysis must test whether the device outperforms the comparator.

**Analysis populations**

The choice of analysis population can influence results. In randomised controlled trials (RCTs), three populations are particularly relevant:

- **Intention-to-treat (ITT):** All participants as randomised, regardless of adherence to study procedures or duration of participation in the study. More reflective of real-world outcomes and more conservative.
- **Per-protocol (PP):** Participants who adhered to the protocol. Reflects outcomes under ideal conditions.
- **Safety population:** All participants exposed to the device, used for adverse event reporting.

These terms come from RCT methodology, but the principle applies more broadly. In non-randomised or diagnostic accuracy studies, equivalent concepts should be defined. For example:

- "All enrolled" or "all usable data" set
- A restricted dataset excluding major protocol deviations or unusable data
- A safety population where relevant, particularly for therapeutic or invasive devices.

**Addressing confounding**

Confounding refers to the systematic differences between groups that influence outcomes. Well-designed and executed RCTs are the most robust way to minimise confounding and provide the strongest evidence for causal effects, but not all medical device studies are randomised.

Observational studies, registries, and real-world evaluations are often central to the evidence package. Here are strategies to adjust for confounding with these study types:

- **Stratification** - Analyse results separately within subgroups defined by key confounders (e.g. age, sex, disease severity).
- **Regression adjustment** - Include confounders as covariates in regression models (logistic, linear, Cox, negative binomial), to estimate the independent effect of the device.
- **Propensity scores** - Estimate the probability of receiving the device given observed covariates, then:
  - Match patients across groups (propensity score matching).
  - Weight participants to balance groups (inverse probability weighting).
  - Stratify analyses by propensity score bands.

### Expected Content: Statistical Design and Analysis

**Statistical analysis plan**

In your statistical analysis plan, specify the population being analysed (e.g. all participants enrolled, only participants who adhered to the protocol, only participants exposed to the device).

Outline the statistical methods used to analyse data, including sample size calculation, handling of missing data, subgroup analysis (if applicable) and bias control methods.

**Methods of analysis**

The choice of statistical method should reflect both the type of data being analysed and the hypothesis under test.

- Binary outcomes (e.g. correct classification, adverse event occurrence) are proportions. Confidence intervals should be calculated using binomial methods, and comparisons may use chi-square tests, Fisher's exact test, or logistic regression.
- Continuous outcomes (e.g. time to diagnosis, usability scores) can be summarised with means and standard deviations and analysed using t-tests, ANOVA, or regression models.
- Time-to-event outcomes (e.g. survival, time to recurrence) are typically analysed using Kaplan-Meier methods and Cox proportional hazards models.

Diagnostic accuracy studies require particular care. They typically involve comparing the device's results against a reference standard to determine sensitivity, specificity, and related measures.

When a comparator method is also applied to the same patients (e.g. clinician reading vs device output), this creates paired binary classifications, and paired statistical tests such as McNemar's are appropriate.

**Methods to reduce or adjust for confounding**

- Stratification: Analyse results separately within subgroups defined by key confounders.
- Regression adjustment: Include confounders as covariates in regression models.
- Propensity scores: Estimate the probability of receiving the device given observed covariates.

**Example methodology**

Below is the outline of a statistical analysis to demonstrate superiority of the sensitivity for medical device software that detects melanoma by analysing skin images.

- **Objective**: Demonstrate superiority of SaMD sensitivity compared to standard care.
- **Significance level (alpha)**: 0.05 (two-sided test).
- **Power (1-beta)**: 90%.
- **Minimum detectable difference**: 7% improvement in sensitivity.
- **Expected SaMD sensitivity**: 92% (based on internal validation data on 500 images).
- **Expected standard care sensitivity**: 85% (from Smith et al., 2023, meta-analysis of dermatologist performance, n=15 studies).
- **Anticipated absolute difference**: 7% improvement in sensitivity.
- **Justification**: A 7% absolute improvement translates to detecting approximately 1 additional melanoma for every 14 true cases, which is clinically significant given melanoma mortality risk.
- **Melanoma prevalence in study population**: 8% (based on referral patterns to dermatology clinics for suspicious lesions, Jones et al., 2022). This means for every 100 patients, we expect ~8 true melanoma cases.
- **Sample size calculation**: Using McNemar's test for paired proportions (same patients evaluated by both methods). Required sample size: 312 patients with suspicious lesions. Expected melanoma cases: ~25 (312 x 0.08).

### Expected Content: Investigator's Brochure

The clinical investigator's brochure provides investigators and regulatory authorities with the rationale for the clinical investigation, evidence of preclinical and clinical safety and performance, and all essential instructions for the safe use of the device.

Here is an outline of what ought to be included:

- **Brochure information**
  - Date and version.
  - Authors.
  - Confidentiality statement.
- **Device information**
  - Intended purpose.
  - Intended clinical performance of the device in the context of its use in the investigation.
  - Device classification with version number.
  - Manufacturer details.
- **Clinical investigation plan**
  - Refer to the plan that describes the purpose, procedures, and conduct of the investigation.
  - Details of any amendments made to the clinical investigation plan, if applicable.
- **Literature review**
  - Details of the literature search conducted.
  - Summary of the literature findings.
- **Software verification**
  - Summary of the confirmation of software verification activities.
- **IFU and training**
  - Provide the instructions for use (IFU) that will accompany the device during the investigation.
  - Describe any plans for training.
- **Preclinical information**
  - Summarise the tests and validations conducted to justify the device's use in human subjects.
  - Include clinical evidence of the device being used for its intended purpose if available.
- **Adverse effects, contraindications, warnings**
  - Provide details of any known adverse device effects.
  - Provide details of known contraindications for the device.
  - Provide details of known precautions.
- **Risk management**
  - A summary of any benefit-risk analyses, residual risks and any undesirable side effects.
- **Statement of conformity**
  - List the international standards the investigation complies with (e.g. ISO 14155:2020).
  - Describe how the device will fully demonstrate conformity to all applicable Annex I requirements over time.
  - Include a statement of conformity for the applicable regulation for your device (e.g. Regulation EU 2017/745).
