# Audits

Audits assess your quality management system (QMS) against ISO 13485 and applicable regulations. They also verify that your processes match your documented system.

Audits happen during initial certification and throughout the certification cycle.

## How Audits Work

The exact format depends on scope and what needs verification. Most audits follow this pattern:

1. **Remote or on-site:** If no on-site verification is needed, we audit remotely. For remote audits, we use a Zoom call and a dedicated chat channel.
2. **Opening meeting:** Confirm scope, schedule, and how we'll raise findings.
3. **Evidence review:** We review objective evidence uploaded in the Scarlet app. We ask questions in the chat as we go. We may pull people into the call for clarifications.
4. **Closing meeting:** We present findings and agree on timelines for responses.

We may adapt the agenda based on company size, device type, and audit stage.

## Types of Audits

You may see these audit types (not always in this order):

- **QMS Stage 1 audit:** An initial assessment of the state of your management system that determines readiness for a Stage 2 audit. You may be required to repeat a Stage 1 audit. See the Deep Dive: Stage 1 Audit section for more details.
- **QMS Stage 2 audit:** A further assessment of your management system and its alignment with the applicable standard and regulations. Following a Stage 2 audit, Scarlet may either approve the quality management system (subject to identified nonconformities being addressed appropriately) and issue a certificate, or refuse to approve your management system. See the Deep Dive: Stage 2 Audit section for more details.
- **QMS Close-out Audit:** An optional audit to verify that nonconformities have been addressed.
- **Technical documentation review:** If applicable, an assessment of your technical documentation. This is required for CE/UKCA marking for Class Im, Class IIa, Class IIb, and Class III devices.
- **Surveillance audits:** Carried out in 12 month intervals during the certification cycle. Following each surveillance audit, Scarlet may either recommend continuation of the certification cycle or suspend your certificate.
- **Unannounced audits:** Performed at least once every 5 years (or at least once every 3 years for class III devices). Scarlet may also perform an unannounced audit in responding to a complaint and may include suppliers and/or subcontractors.
- **Recertification audit:** Carried out as your certificate nears expiry, at the end of the certification cycle (e.g. 4 months before your certificate expires). Based on the findings throughout the audit cycle, Scarlet may either recommend renewal of the certificate or refuse to renew it.

During your audits, Scarlet will review technical documentation generated from post-market surveillance activities. This may include the Post Market Clinical Follow-up (PMCF) report and the Periodic Safety Update Report (PSUR).

For more information on these documents, please see the Expected documents: Post market clinical follow-up (PMCF) report and Expected documents: Periodic safety update report (PSUR) sections.

## Attendance Requirements

- **Opening and closing meetings:** Attendance is required.
- **Offline parts:** No attendance is required. Just make sure you keep an eye on the chat channel to answer any questions promptly.
- **Clarifications and discussions:** If further clarification on a question posted in the chat is needed, the audit team will request you to join the call. The request will be sent through the chat.
- **Interviews:** Relevant functions attend the interviews at agreed timings.

## Deep Dive: Stage 1 Audit

### The Purpose of the Stage 1 Audit

The purpose of the Stage 1 quality management system (QMS) audit is to determine the readiness of the QMS for the Stage 2 audit.

The objectives of the Stage 1 audit are:

- Review the manufacturer's management system documented information.
- Evaluate the manufacturer's site-specific conditions and to undertake discussions with the manufacturer's personnel to determine the preparedness for Stage 2.
- Review the manufacturer's status and understanding regarding requirements of the standard, in particular with respect to the identification of key performance or significant aspects, processes, objectives and operation of the management system.
- Obtain necessary information regarding the scope of the management system, including:
  - the manufacturer's site(s)
  - processes and equipment used
  - levels of controls established (particularly in case of multisite manufacturers)
  - applicable statutory and regulatory requirements.
- Review the allocation of resources for Stage 2 and agree the details of Stage 2 with the manufacturer.
- Provide a focus for planning Stage 2 by gaining a sufficient understanding of the manufacturer's management system and site operations in the context of the management system standard or other normative document.
- Evaluate if the internal audits and management reviews are being planned and performed, and that the level of implementation of the management system substantiates that the manufacturer is ready for Stage 2.

### Information Reviewed in the Stage 1 Audit

To achieve the above objective, the Scarlet assessment team will review whether your QMS is sufficiently documented and shows evidence of early implementation. The focus at this stage is primarily on your procedures -- we assess whether the necessary procedures are defined, coherent, and appropriately aligned with regulatory expectations.

While you are not required to upload records of every QMS process, some evidence of implementation is expected. In particular, records of internal audits and management reviews must be available. For other processes, supporting records are generally not needed in advance, but may be sampled during the audit to confirm that the QMS is operational and progressing beyond documentation alone.

The following areas will be reviewed:

- General QMS requirements
- Quality Manual, Quality Policy and Quality Objectives
- Management and Management Review
- Responsibility and Authority
- Competence
- Infrastructure
- Product Realisation
- Medical Device File
- Communication
- Purchasing
- Feedback & PMS
- Internal Auditing
- Monitoring and Measurement
- Analysis of Data
- Nonconforming Product
- Improvement

### After the Stage 1 Audit

Following your audit, the Scarlet assessment team will provide you with a report of the audit. The report will include areas of concern and/or nonconformities.

We need an official response to Areas of Concern (AoC), describing the changes and referring to any updated documents. Scarlet will review these before scheduling the Stage 2 Audit.

## Deep Dive: Stage 2 Audit

### The Purpose of the Stage 2 Audit

The purpose of the Stage 2 audit is to examine how well the management system has been implemented and how effective it is.

The objectives of the Stage 2 audit are:

- Verify that the management system complies with all requirements of the applicable standard(s) and other relevant normative documents.
- Evaluate how top management demonstrates leadership and commitment, including the establishment, communication, and implementation of relevant policies and objectives.
- Assess how the organisation measures, monitors, reports, and reviews its performance against defined objectives and targets, ensuring alignment with the expectations of the applicable standard.
- Evaluate the management system's ability to meet legal, regulatory, and contractual obligations, and assess its performance in doing so.
- Examine the effectiveness of the organisation's operational controls, ensuring that core processes are managed and functioning as intended.
- Confirm that internal audits are conducted regularly and that management reviews are used effectively to drive continual improvement and strategic alignment.

### Information Reviewed in the Stage 2 Audit

To achieve the above objective, the Scarlet assessment team will review the following information related to your QMS:

- Information and evidence about conformity to all requirements of the applicable management system standard or other normative documents.
- Performance monitoring, measurement, analysis, and evaluation, including reporting and review against key performance indicators and quality objectives.
- The manufacturer's management system performance and its ability to meet applicable statutory, regulatory and contractual requirements.
- Operational control of the manufacturer's processes, including the management of outsourced processes and risk-based approach where applicable.
- The implementation and effectiveness of the Internal audit programme and management review process.
- Top management responsibility and involvement, including the establishment, communication, and alignment of policies, objectives, and roles within the organisation.
- The effectiveness of corrective action and continual improvement processes, including how nonconformities are identified, investigated, and resolved to prevent recurrence.
- The organisation's approach to customer focus, including how customer feedback, satisfaction data, and complaints are monitored and used to drive improvements.
- The control and management of documented information, including how quality documents and records are created, updated, maintained, and retained in accordance with the requirements of the standard and your QMS.

### After the Stage 2 Audit

Following your audit, the Scarlet assessment team will provide you with a report of the audit.

Areas of non-conformity require an official response from you. For more information about how to approach responses to non-conformities, please see the Deep Dive: Audit Findings section on responding to nonconformities in your QMS assessment.

## Deep Dive: Audit Findings

### Types of Findings

When our assessors review your submitted data, they classify their findings into four types:

- **Compliance:** This category applies when you have provided evidence of meeting the regulations. While we will record your compliance, no additional feedback will be provided.
- **Areas of Concern:** An important part of assessing a manufacturer's Quality Management System (QMS) in a Stage 1 audit is identifying potential risks and concerns before moving to Stage 2. These risks and concerns are identified and raised as Areas of Concern. While not classified as nonconformities, these concerns serve as high-level indications of potential compliance issues that require attention. An Area of Concern is an issue that, if left unresolved, could develop into one or more nonconformities during Stage 2. These concerns serve as early warnings and highlight weaknesses or gaps in a manufacturer's QMS that must be addressed to ensure smooth progression to full certification. All Stage 1 AoC must be addressed ahead of Stage 2 audit.
- **Observations:** Here, your provided data is compliant, but there may be room for improvement or potential issues that could arise if not addressed.
- **Nonconformities (NCs):** NCs are recorded when your provided data fails to comply with regulations. These can be major or minor. Multiple minor NCs in the same area may be indicative of a major failing, and hence a major NC can be issued against that one topic/area, instead of multiple minors.

### Your Data, Your Responsibility

If we identify a finding and you believe that miscommunication resulted from the Scarlet submission process, please consider the following in your response:

**Determining Root Cause:**

Was the issue due to:
- failure to conduct a compliant process?
- failure to record the process conducted accurately?

**Addressing Root Causes:**

- Identify why the conducted process didn't meet requirements (e.g., wrong personnel assigned, missed considerations).
- Rectify the root cause and update the data accordingly.

**If you feel as though the Scarlet submission process is causing a misinterpretation of your data:**

- Identify the aspect of our system causing misrepresentation.
- Provide this feedback to us so we can address this.

Note: You have sole responsibility for the data you provide to us. It must be accurate, complete and demonstrate your compliance with the regulations.

### Feedback Rounds

You will have three opportunities to resolve a nonconformity, one after each review.

If your response is still deemed unacceptable after the third review, we may decide to terminate the conformity assessment. We hope to avoid this outcome by focusing on high-quality data and feedback to ensure efficient assessments.

However, the regulations require us to rigorously protect our impartiality. Our status as a notified body is dependent on it. Unlimited rounds of feedback could result in:

- over-familiarity with your data, to the extent that we are no longer critically assessing the facts presented,
- criticisms of "coaching" you toward corrections, that we are then required to (but cannot) impartially assess.

The total number of feedback rounds is at the discretion of our assessors. It may vary depending on assessment complexity and circumstances.

### Difference: Corrections, Root Cause Analysis vs Corrective Actions

Here is an analogy to explain the difference between Corrections, Root Cause Analysis and Corrective actions:

- **A Correction** is like applying a bandage to a cut -- it's immediate and localised to stop the symptom (nonconformity). You stop the bleeding and protect the wound (eliminate the detected nonconformity), but it doesn't address why the person got injured in the first place.
- **A Root Cause Analysis (RCA)** figures out why you got the cut in the first place -- was the knife dull, the cutting technique wrong, or the lighting poor? If so, why was the knife dull, the cutting technique wrong, or the lighting poor?
- **The Corrective action** fixes the issues identified in the root cause analysis so you won't get cut again (it prevents the recurrence of the nonconformity).

A properly conducted RCA is essential for effective Corrective Actions. For example, if the RCA concludes that the knife was dull and stops there, a Corrective Action to sharpen the knife may appear sufficient. But this is more akin to a Correction -- it addresses the immediate issue with the knife, not the underlying cause. When the knife inevitably becomes dull again, the nonconformity may recur.

An effective RCA goes deeper to identify why a dull knife was used. For instance, it might reveal these two root causes which would enable effective Corrective Actions that prevent recurrence:

1. The knife was dull because the procedure doesn't include knife maintenance; **Corrective action:** Implement a procedure with assigned responsibility for sharpening, and a defined sharpening schedule.
2. A dull knife was used because the training doesn't include knife safety and how to check sharpness. **Corrective action:** Update training to include knife safety and how to check sharpness before use.

### Root Cause Analysis: What We Look For

A poorly conducted RCA often results from superficial analysis, a lack of evidence, and poor methodology. It can lead to ineffective corrective actions, recurring nonconformities, and spreading noncompliance.

A structured, data-driven, and systemic approach enables finding the true root cause(s) of identified nonconformities. This will ensure effective corrective actions and regulatory compliance.

**What we look for:**

- Is the RCA methodology appropriate for the NC's complexity? (5 Whys Analysis, Ishikawa (Fishbone) Diagram, Fault Tree Analysis (FTA), etc.)
- Are the identified root causes specific rather than symptoms of the nonconformity?
- Does the analysis consider systemic issues, not just isolated incidents?
- Have all potential root causes been identified?

**Common mistakes in poorly conducted RCAs:**

| Common mistake | Example of a bad RCA | How to fix it |
|---|---|---|
| Stopping at symptoms instead of identifying the true root cause | "The product failed because an employee made an error." | Ask why error was possible; check training, instructions, process design |
| Using a weak RCA methodology or none at all | No structured approach. Guessing. Relying on only a single "Why" to determine the cause of the NC. | Use structured RCA methods, e.g. 5 Whys for simple issues, Ishikawa (Fishbone) Diagram for complex issues, Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) for design/process failures. |
| Identifying a single cause instead of a systemic issue | "The nonconformity was due to a supplier mistake." | Why did the supplier mistake occur? Was the supplier evaluation sufficient? Was purchase requirements and incoming inspection sufficient? Are similar mistakes happening elsewhere in the supply chain? |
| Jumping to conclusions without data | "We believe the issue happened because of a system failure." (No evidence provided.) | Collect data (e.g., output logs, system records, trend analysis). Perform structured failure analysis before making assumptions. |
| Failing to Consider Risk Management (ISO 14971) | "This issue is minor, so we won't take further action." | Perform a risk assessment (Is patient safety impacted? Does it affect compliance?). Even "minor" issues can be early indicators of major failures. |

### Corrections: What We Look For

The correction eliminates the nonconformity and addresses any symptoms.

**What we look for:**

- Does the correction consider any temporary adjustments required to immediately address symptoms?
- Does the correction consider any immediate updates required to eliminate the NC?
- Does the correction prevent the further spread of the issue?
- Are corrections implemented quickly and effectively?
- Are affected stakeholders informed if needed?

### Corrective Actions: What We Look For

**What we look for:**

- Does the action permanently address the root cause?
- Are preventive measures included (e.g., improved training, procedural changes)?
- Are actions integrated into the QMS (e.g., SOP updates, risk management review)?
- Has a clear effectiveness check been defined?

**Common mistakes:**

- Implementing weak corrective actions that only address symptoms (e.g., retraining staff without fixing unclear procedures).
- Only addressing the current issue without system-level changes leads to recurrence.

### Implemented Actions: What We Look For

**What we look for:**

- Objective evidence of implementation (e.g., revised procedures, training records, updated work instructions).
- Actions have been implemented according to the accepted plan.

### Effectiveness Verification: What We Look For

**What we look for:**

- Is there objective evidence that the effectiveness has been verified?
- Check effectiveness metrics, such as:
  - Trend analysis (Are similar nonconformities decreasing?)
  - CAPA follow-up audits (Does the issue reoccur?)
  - Employee feedback (Is training effective?)
- Check that the effectiveness verification has been implemented according to the accepted plan.

**Common mistakes:**

- Closing the NC without verifying effectiveness over time.

## FAQs

### When can I schedule my stage 1 audit?

Scarlet will work closely with you on your availability for a stage 1 audit once we are closer to accepting your application. Once your application has been officially accepted, we will be able to confirm the audit date with you.

Scarlet will aim to offer a stage 1 audit within two weeks of the application being accepted.

### What are you expecting to see from us before audits?

**Before your stage 1 audit:**
- You will have already submitted the necessary documentation as part of your application.
- Prepare a short 10 minute presentation about your company as an introduction to the Scarlet audit team. This will be presented during the opening meeting.

**Before your stage 2 audit:**
- Ensure that you have addressed all Areas of Concern raised in the stage 1 audit.
- Submit updated documentation as a result of addressing the Areas of Concern. This should be provided to the audit team at least 2 working days prior to a stage 2 audit.

**Before your Surveillance audit:**
- Submit all the necessary documentation. All changes should be provided to the audit team at least 5 working days prior to a surveillance audit.
- Prepare a short 10 minute presentation about your company and any changes since the last audit relevant to the Scarlet audit team. This will be presented during the opening meeting.

### Do we need to formally respond to Areas of Concern raised in the stage 1 audit before the stage 2 audit?

We don't need an official response to Areas of Concern (AoC), but you should address the AoC in your QMS as you see fit, describe the changes and refer to any updated documents. Scarlet will re-assess these in the stage 2 Audit.

> Note: Depending on the amount and nature of concerns raised, Scarlet may need to schedule a repeat stage 1 audit. We will inform you if this is the case.

### What is the time needed between completing stage 1 and scheduling stage 2 audits?

The maximum amount of time permitted between stage 1 and stage 2 audits is 6 months. Other than that, the time between completing stage 1 and stage 2 is flexible, based on how long a manufacturer needs to address any AoCs raised.

As per ISO 17021 requirements, which Scarlet must fulfil as a notified body:

> In determining the interval between stage 1 and stage 2, consideration shall be given to the needs of the client to resolve areas of concern identified during stage 1... If any significant changes which would impact the management system occur, the certification body shall also consider the need to repeat all or part of stage 1.
>
> -- ISO 17021 Section 9.3.1.2.4

### Can we update or make any changes to our submission between stage 1 and stage 2 audits?

Whilst we expect that changes will be made to address any areas of concern, any major changes, such as a structural improvement to your QMS (e.g. creation of new documented procedures, deletion of existing documented procedures, in-sourcing of a previously outsourced process) will require us to evaluate whether a second stage 1 audit is necessary before moving to stage 2.

### Who needs to attend the audit?

Top level management needs to attend the opening and closing meetings at the start and end of the audit.

Responsible function(s) must be available to reply to questions when their area is being audited.

### Do we need to be free all day and what are the expectations for our availability?

You are expected to have key personnel available to address questions and provide relevant information as needed throughout the audit. This personnel does not have to be on the Zoom call for the duration of the day, but should be able to respond to questions with a reasonable response time (ideally between 15-30 minutes depending on the complexity of the question/answer).

If key team members are not available for the entire day, we will do our best to work with you to schedule topics around availability. Please let us know well in advance so that we can adjust the audit schedule accordingly.

### What are the timings/order of events throughout the day?

The Scarlet team will share an audit plan before the audit with the timings of scheduled topics and sessions throughout the day(s), as well as other key details.
