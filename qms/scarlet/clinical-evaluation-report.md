# Clinical Evaluation Report

Create a clinical evaluation report that summarises your evidence generation activities, supporting the safety and performance of your device.

The clinical evaluation is the culmination of your evidence generation activities. The basis of the document will be the clinical evaluation plan, where these activities are outlined.

Findings will inform the assessment of the risk-benefit ratio and will feed into the risk management report. Gaps in evidence can be addressed through post-market activities, detailed in the post-market clinical follow-up plan.

## Minimum Requirements

This is a beefy list but it's well worth spending the time to cover these points as you should have some compelling clinical benefit claims to shout about, providing the evidence substantiates them.

- **Basic information** - Describe the device, unique identifiers (e.g. Basic UDI-DI), and risk classification. List the names, qualifications, and roles of the individuals who authored and reviewed the CER.
- **Clinical evaluation methodology** - Outline the structured approach used to identify, select, appraise, and analyse clinical data.
- **Data summary and appraisal** - Provide a summary of the clinical data sets included in the evaluation, with findings for each clinical question addressed, before presenting the outcomes of the critical appraisal of each data set.
- **Clinical benefit and performance analysis** - For each clinical benefit claim, link to outcome measures and supporting data, before describing how performance is demonstrated.
- **Safety analysis** - Summarise adverse event data and identify any new or emerging risks. State the frequency and severity of events in context of intended use, integrating findings with the risk management file.
- **State of the art evaluation** - Describe the current clinical landscape relevant to the device's intended use, including established treatment alternatives and clinical guidelines or standards that define best practice. Does its safety and performance meet or exceed the current benchmark?
- **Benefit-risk assessment** - Justify the clinical acceptability of any residual risks in light of the state of the art, available alternatives, and the current level of medical knowledge.
- **Gaps and future activities** - Identify evidence gaps, with a justification as to why additional data is required, and planned follow-up activities to support evidence generation during the device's lifecycle.
- **Conclusions** - Provide a statement confirming whether the clinical evidence demonstrates achievement of intended clinical benefits and an acceptable benefit-risk profile. Summarise any conditions or limitations to claims and finally, state whether these findings can be included in the *Declaration of Conformity.*

See the Deep dive section for more information on each of these elements, including examples of what to include.

## Common Pitfalls

When appraising data, several pitfalls can undermine the validity and utility of your analysis. It pains us to see inadequate analyses whose data does not evidence the clinical benefit claims made. Avoid the following to ensure that this time investment yields meaningful findings.

> **Warning:**
>
> **Lack of systematic quality assessment** is perhaps the most significant pitfall. Without using structured tools, like GRADE or QUADAS-2, you risk accepting low-quality evidence as equivalent to robust studies. The absence of such a tool may give equal weight to a small pilot study with 50 data points from a single clinic and a multi-center validation study with 10,000 data points from diverse populations.
>
> **Ignoring the intended use environment and population** represents a critical gap. A melanoma SaMD validated exclusively on Fitzpatrick skin types I-III may perform poorly on darker skin tones, yet literature reviews often don't systematically extract and analyse demographic subgroup data.
>
> **Inadequate handling of heterogeneity** in study designs and populations is another major issue. Melanoma detection studies vary in their reference standards (histopathology vs. expert dermatologist diagnosis), lesion selection criteria (enriched datasets with high prevalence vs. real-world populations), and patient demographics (skin phototypes, anatomical locations).
>
> **Publication bias and selective outcome reporting** often go unaddressed. Positive studies showing high accuracy are more likely to be published than negative results. For melanoma SaMD, this creates an inflated perception of algorithm performance.
>
> **Overlooking temporal and technological evolution** of the field can make your state-of-the-art assessment outdated before completion. Melanoma detection algorithms have evolved rapidly from simple rule-based systems to deep learning models.

## Desired Format

We really want to review your documents and these tips will enable us to do that:

- Upload narrative documents as text files (.DOCX or .PDF) formats for written reports.
- Upload spreadsheets (.XLSX) files for detailed or data-intensive evidence, like literature searches or appraisals of clinical data.
- Keep your files searchable, avoiding scanned PDFs or locked documents.

While not a requirement, we love to see a traceability matrix that connects clinical benefit claims to related clinical performance data. This has the following constituents:

- **Clinical benefit** - The claimed positive impact of a device on the health of an individual, patient management or public health.
- **Outcome measure** - The quantifiable metric used to assess the effectiveness, safety, or performance of a medical intervention.
- **Clinical performance claim** - A claimed ability of the device, resulting from its characteristics, to achieve its intended purpose, thereby leading to a clinical benefit.
- **State-of-the-art performance benchmark** - Related to some state-of-the-art data from similar devices, alternative pathways, or alternative treatments.
- **Clinical evaluation activity** - Clinical performance data generated by testing the device under evaluation or an equivalent device, in its intended target population and for its intended use.
- **Clinical evaluation activity endpoint** - The relevant clinical evaluation variable that was measured to determine the effect of the intervention.
- **Clinical performance data** - Clinical performance findings from the clinical evaluation activity.

## Deep Dive

### Expected Content: Basic Information

Key device details include:

- Device name.
- Unique identifiers (e.g. Basic UDI-DI).
- Risk classification.

In addition to including standard device information, it is important to outline the context of the device and scope of the report:

- Explain the intended purpose and indications for use that are relevant to this evaluation.
- Indicate which GSPRs from Annex I are addressed within the scope of this CER.
- State whether the evaluators have verified the accuracy of the report, and include confirmation of their sign-off (per Clause 11 and A10).
- Include declarations of interest and provide a justification for the evaluators' suitability, aligned with MEDDEV 2.7/1 Rev. 4 Section 6.4.

### Expected Content: Clinical Evaluation Methodology

This is the core of the document and where a lot of your efforts will be directed.

- Outline the methods used to identify data including literature searches, internal data sources (e.g. clinical investigations, real-world evidence) and external data sources (e.g. data registries).
- Detail the search strategy: databases queried with rationale for selection; search terms, filters and timeframes applied; and screening methods with documentation of included/excluded studies.
  - Any additional techniques such as hand-searching or grey literature review should also be included.
- Explain how studies or data sources were selected or excluded, based on relevance to:
  - The device under evaluation or equivalent devices.
  - Intended use, target population, and clinical context.
  - Study design quality and applicability.
- Consider illustrating the selection of articles in your literature review using a PRISMA flow diagram.
- Outline the measures taken to minimise bias and address uncertainties or limitations in the data, this may be addressed using a structured tool to evaluate the methodological quality of a study.
- Provide an overview of the evaluation methodology, with reference to:
  - Alignment with MEDDEV 2.7/1 Rev. 4.
  - Traceability of conclusions to supporting evidence.
  - Any assumptions made by the evaluators, and how these were justified.

### Expected Content: Data Summary and Appraisal

Your appraisal should demonstrate how the evidence collectively supports the device's intended use, claims, and conformity with relevant GSPRs.

#### Overview of Included Data

Provide a summary of the clinical data sets included in the evaluation, based on your identification and selection methodology. This may include:

- Manufacturer-sponsored clinical investigations.
- Published clinical literature.
- Post-market clinical follow-up (PMCF) studies.
- Real-world evidence or internal documentation (e.g. complaint logs, vigilance data).

#### Results of Data Appraisal

The results of the data appraisal should include:

- Assessment of methodological quality (e.g. study design robustness, completeness of reporting).
- Scientific validity of results.
- Relevance to the device under evaluation and its intended clinical context.
- Weight assigned to each data set in the overall evidence hierarchy.
- Identification of any limitations, uncertainties, or potential bias in the included evidence.

Your approach to data appraisal will have been detailed in the *Clinical evaluation plan* and may utilise a standardised framework (e.g. GRADE) that covers the above elements.

If applicable, briefly describe any excluded data sets that may be relevant to the evaluation. Reference your appraisal criteria and include a justification for exclusion e.g. low-quality studies, non-equivalent devices, outdated comparators.

#### Summary of Findings

When summarising findings, consider grouping them by: type of evidence, performance or safety outcome, target population or indication. Where appropriate, link findings to the specific clinical claims or endpoints defined in the *Clinical evaluation plan.*

### Expected Content: Clinical Benefit and Performance Analysis

To assess whether claimed benefits and performance are substantiated, link evidence to clinical outcomes.

- State claimed clinical benefits (link to CEP).
- For each benefit:
  - Link to outcome measures and supporting data.
  - Describe how performance is demonstrated.
- Include any variability or subgroup findings.
- Explain how benefit-risk acceptability was assessed in light of the state of the art.
- State whether predefined acceptance criteria were met.

### Expected Content: Safety Analysis

To evaluate clinical safety, summarise adverse event data and identify any new or emerging risks:

- Known or observed adverse events and SADEs.
- Unanticipated events or safety signals from post-market data.
- Frequency and severity of events in context of intended use.
- Comparative safety (vs equivalent devices or alternatives).
- Integration with risk management findings.

### Expected Content: State of the Art

The state of the art provides a benchmark for assessing the acceptability of the device's benefit-risk profile and its clinical performance and safety.

Include the following information in this section:

- **Summary of current knowledge and clinical practice**: Describe the current clinical landscape relevant to the device's intended use, including:
  - Established treatment alternatives
  - Accepted clinical techniques and procedures
  - Relevant comparator technologies
- **Reference to applicable guidelines and standards**: Identify the clinical guidelines, scientific publications, and harmonised standards that define best practice in the relevant medical field. Ensure sources are recent, reliable, and appropriately cited.
- **Comparison with the device under evaluation**: Describe how the device performs relative to the state of the art, including:
  - Whether its safety and performance meet or exceed the current benchmark
  - Any areas where the device may offer clinical advantages or present additional risks
- **Acceptability of benefit-risk profile**: Justify the clinical acceptability of any residual risks in light of the state of the art, available alternatives, and the current level of medical knowledge.

The expectation is that the SOTA assessment is kept up to date and reflects the time at which the clinical evaluation is being performed. This activity functions to support conclusions on GSPR conformity and provide context for the benefit-risk evaluation.

### Expected Content: Benefit-risk Assessment

To justify conformity under MDR, assess the totality of evidence and its support for benefit-risk acceptability:

- Summary of how benefits outweigh risks.
- Sufficiency of evidence for each intended use and population.
- Adequacy of follow-up duration to assess long-term outcomes.
- Performance in comparison to the state of the art.
- Highlight any gaps, residual risks, or uncertainties and state whether these will be addressed through PMCF or other PMS measures.

### Expected Content: Gaps and Future Activities

Identify evidence gaps and plan follow-up activities to support evidence generation during the device's lifecycle:

- Limitations of current data or areas of residual uncertainty.
- PMCF activities planned or ongoing (linked to PMCF Plan).
- Other measures:
  - Registry participation.
  - Usability or simulated use studies.
  - Periodic literature review or data updates.
- Justification of why additional data is required or how gaps are managed.

#### Clinical Investigation

Indicate whether a clinical investigation was performed as part of the evidence base. If not performed, provide a justification for the absence of such data in line with Article 61.

If a clinical investigation was conducted, summarise the findings and reference the *Clinical investigation report*.
