# Clinical Evaluation Plan

Create a clinical evaluation plan that outlines your evidence generation activities, detailing how clinical benefit claims will be substantiated.

The CEP defines a structured process for the continuous generation, assessment, and analysis of clinical data. It forms the foundation for all clinical evaluation activities throughout the device lifecycle, demonstrating the device's conformity with safety and performance requirements.

> **Tip:** Schedule a clinical foundations structured dialogue to help ensure that you have a systematic approach to your clinical evaluation activities.

## Minimum Requirements

The clinical evaluation plan should define the following:

- **Clinical evaluators** - Include their credentials, declarations of interest and justify their suitability with relevant qualifications listed.
- **Clinical benefit claims** - Specify meaningful, patient-relevant clinical outcomes that will determine the endpoints of your clinical data generation activities.
- **Outcome measures** - Describe the outcome measures that will be used to assess whether the intended clinical benefits are being achieved.
- **Alternative management and SOTA** - Describe the approach used to determine what constitutes the current state of the art, including a literature search and a review clinical guidelines or applicable standards.
- **Appraisal of data sources** - Detail how clinical data will be identified, selected, and appraised for inclusion in the evaluation.
- **Detailing planned activities** - Describe the type of activity (e.g. literature review, clinical investigation, PMCF), the questions it aims to answer and the methodology to be used.
- **Benefit-risk assessment approach** - Describe the approach used to assess the acceptability of the benefit-risk profile of the device, including how clinical benefits will be weighed against known and residual risks.

See the Deep dive section for more information on each of these elements, including examples of what to include.

## Common Pitfalls

> **Warning:** Ironically, manufacturers often struggle to articulate meaningful and patient-relevant clinical benefit claims, even when the potential impact of their device on patient care is obvious!
>
> It's worth emphasising that any claim made needs to be substantiated by data generated through clinical evaluation activities. A clinical benefit claim must therefore be **measurable**, in addition to being **meaningful** and **patient-relevant**.
>
> Let's consider a device that provides personalised exercise and nutrition recommendations to patients with type 2 diabetes for the purpose of optimising the management of that condition. The device uses inputs like weight measurements, blood biomarkers, and daily questionnaire responses to generate the recommendations. Here are some examples of low-quality clinical benefit claims for our proposed device:
>
> - *"The device will improve the patient's management of their diabetes."*
>   - While improving the management of diabetes is an admirable goal, by what measure are they going to determine whether diabetic management has been improved?
> - *"The device will increase the patient's awareness of their blood sugar control."*
>   - Patient awareness of their blood sugar control might be important, but it's not explicitly clear why that might be beneficial when considering the intended purpose (diabetes management).
> - *"The device will provide a better patient experience."* OR *"The device will free up resources for patients with more complicated health needs."*
>   - Of course, improving patient experience and freeing up healthcare resources is laudable, but it might be pointless if the actual condition for which the device is intended cannot be improved.
>
> With all these factors in mind, a higher-quality clinical benefit claim might look something like:
>
> *"Patients, on average, will achieve their target HbA1c level within 6 months."*

## Desired Format

We really want to review your documents and these tips will enable us to do that:

- Upload narrative documents as text files (.DOCX or .PDF) formats for written reports.
- Upload spreadsheets (.XLSX) files for detailed or data-intensive evidence, like literature searches or appraisals of clinical data.
- Keep your files searchable, avoiding scanned PDFs or locked documents.

While not a requirement, we love to see a traceability matrix that connects clinical benefit claims to related clinical performance data. This has the following constituents:

- **Clinical benefit** - The claimed positive impact of a device on the health of an individual, patient management or public health.
- **Outcome measure** - The quantifiable metric used to assess the effectiveness, safety, or performance of a medical intervention.
- **Clinical performance claim** - A claimed ability of the device, resulting from its characteristics, to achieve its intended purpose, thereby leading to a clinical benefit.
- **State-of-the-art performance benchmark** - Related to some state-of-the-art data from similar devices, alternative pathways, or alternative treatments.
- **Clinical evaluation activity** - Clinical performance data generated by testing the device under evaluation or an equivalent device, in its intended target population and for its intended use.
- **Clinical evaluation activity endpoint** - The relevant clinical evaluation variable that was measured to determine the effect of the intervention.
- **Clinical performance data** - Clinical performance findings from the clinical evaluation activity.

## Deep Dive

### Expected Content: Clinical Evaluators

**Qualifications and justification**

The level of expertise should be appropriate to the nature of the device, its clinical application, and its associated risks.

Acceptable qualifications include either:

- A relevant higher degree (e.g. in medicine, life sciences, biomedical engineering) and at least **5 years** of documented professional experience; or
- At least **10 years** of documented professional experience in the relevant field, if a degree is not required for the task.

Include a justification for why each clinical evaluator has been selected. This should reference:

- Their qualifications and academic background.
- Their professional experience relevant to the device.
- Any prior work on similar technologies, therapeutic areas, or evaluations.

If an evaluator's expertise falls short in any area, this should be acknowledged and appropriately justified within the CEP.

**Declaration of interest**

A signed declaration of interest must be obtained from each clinical evaluator to confirm their independence and objectivity. The declaration should include any interests that may influence impartiality (e.g. economic interests, institutional affiliations, personal relationships).

> **Tip:** Template declarations are available from the European Commission and should be kept on record for each evaluator.

### Expected Content: Clinical Benefit Claims

Clinical benefit claims form the basis of your clinical evaluation activities. These claims, provided that they are substantiated by your data generation activities, are the most valuable output of the regulatory process. Consider them a powerful sales tool.

Types of claims include:

- Improved diagnostic accuracy.
  - *Device X improves early melanoma detection compared to standard visual inspection.*
- Enhanced treatment effectiveness.
  - *Patients will achieve therapeutic drug levels within the target range in 80% of treatment cycles, compared to 55% with standard weight-based dosing.*
- Better disease management.
  - *Patients, on average, will achieve their target HbA1c level within 6 months.*
- Reduced risk of adverse events.
  - *Patients will have atrial fibrillation detected an average of 18 months earlier than with standard care, enabling timely anticoagulation therapy.*

Each of the examples above is associated with a patient-relevant and measurable outcome measure. Outcome measures can take several forms including clinical endpoints (e.g. mortality rate), surrogate endpoints (e.g. HbA1c), patient-reported outcomes (e.g. pain severity), or functional measures (e.g. time to diagnosis).

You'll notice that the examples above make reference to standard care. These benchmarks will need to be established through data identification activities: clinical guidelines and literature searches.

Claims can be refined and expanded as you grow the evidence base for your device. Bolder claims can be substantiated by real-world performance data or post-market follow-up studies.

### Expected Content: Outcome Measures

Each benefit identified should have at least one corresponding outcome measure.

For each outcome measure:

- Provide a clear description (e.g. what is being measured)
- Specify the type of outcome (e.g. patient-reported, clinical performance, diagnostic accuracy)
- Justify the appropriateness of the measure for capturing the intended benefit

Outcome measures should be **meaningful, measurable, and relevant** to patients or clinical decision-making. These might include clinical endpoints (e.g. mortality rate), surrogate endpoints (e.g. HbA1c), patient-reported outcomes (e.g. pain severity), or functional measures (e.g. time to diagnosis).

The selected outcome measures should be consistent with the device's intended purpose and aligned with the current clinical state of the art.

### Expected Content: Alternative Management and SOTA

**Alternative management options**

Detailing alternative management options helps to understand the clinical context in which the device is used and supports the benefit-risk assessment. This activity identifies the safety and performance standards for the clinical condition or clinical workflow addressed by your device.

Management options include both:

- **Alternative pathways** - clinical workflows for diagnosis, monitoring, or treatment that fulfil the same intended purpose.
- **Alternative treatments** - specific interventions such as pharmacological therapies, surgical procedures, or non-device-based therapies.

Examples of alternatives may include:

- A diagnostic workflow involving dual radiologist review of breast imaging.
- A pharmaceutical intervention (e.g. metformin for type 2 diabetes).
- A behavioural intervention (e.g. cognitive behavioural therapy for anxiety).
- A home monitoring pathway using community nursing services.

If there are gaps in clinical evidence for the alternatives, or if they are not considered the standard of care, this should be acknowledged and supported with justification.

> This section should align with your discussion of state of the art and inform the comparator selection for clinical evaluation activities (e.g. literature review, benchmarking).

**Clinical state of the art**

The state of the art (SOTA) sets the benchmark for clinical safety and performance expectations. A common misconception is that this represents the most technologically advanced solution to the clinical problem being addressed. The question is actually *what is the current management paradigm for the clinical condition being addressed?*

In addition to drawing from guidelines and standards of care, a structured literature review is typically the approach used to establish the SOTA. This includes detailing databases searched, search terms used, and inclusion/exclusion criteria.

The clinical state of the art may consist of one or more of the following:

- **Alternative clinical pathways** currently used to manage the target condition.
- **Alternative treatments** that represent accepted medical, surgical, or non-interventional options.
- **Similar devices** with comparable intended purpose, technological characteristics, or mode of action.
- **Equivalent devices** meeting the applicable criteria for clinical, technical, and biological equivalence.

Where a combination of these is used to define the state of the art, the rationale should be made clear.

The SOTA review concludes with defining the performance and safety expectations based on existing approaches. This forms the basis for assessing the clinical acceptability of the device and supports the benefit-risk determination in subsequent sections.

### Expected Content: Appraisal of Clinical Data

Not all data is created equal and it is important to separate robust data that can support a clinical benefit claim from the chaff that threatens to undermine this entire process. A standardised framework may help to ensure transparency and reproducibility.

An example of such a framework is GRADE which rates the certainty of evidence and the strength of recommendations made. Cochrane also provides tools that evaluate the risk of bias: RoB 2.0 for randomised controlled trials and ROBINS-I for non-randomised studies.

Manufacturers often develop a hybrid approach that combines elements from established frameworks while addressing MDR-specific requirements. We are framework-agnostic but if there's one thing that we love, it's a systematic approach!

### Expected Content: Detailing Planned Activities

For each clinical evaluation activity, detail:

- The purpose of the activity and the specific questions it aims to answer.
- The methodology to be used.
- The criteria for determining the acceptability of findings.

The activities should be appropriate for the device's risk class, intended use, and data gaps identified. The two examples below illustrate the difference in methodological rigour between a class IIa and a class III device.

**Class IIa**

**Example Device:** A software algorithm that analyses ECG data to detect atrial fibrillation and alerts clinicians

**Appropriate Study Design:** Prospective, single-arm observational study with historical control

- **Population:** 200-300 patients in cardiology clinics with risk factors for AF
- **Intervention:** Clinicians use the SaMD alongside standard care
- **Comparator:** Historical detection rates from the same centres pre-implementation (or published literature benchmarks)
- **Primary endpoint:** Time to AF detection compared to historical data
- **Secondary endpoints:**
  - Diagnostic accuracy (sensitivity/specificity vs. cardiologist review)
  - Usability metrics and user satisfaction
  - Rate of false positives
- **Duration:** 6-12 months
- **Analysis:** Paired comparison of detection rates, subgroup analysis by patient risk profile.

**Class III Device - Clinical Benefit Study**

**Example Device:** A transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR) system

**Appropriate Study Design:** Prospective, multi-centre, randomised controlled trial (RCT) with active comparator

- **Population:** 600-1000 patients with severe symptomatic aortic stenosis at high/intermediate surgical risk
- **Intervention:** New TAVR device
- **Comparator:**
  - Either surgical AVR (if claiming superiority/non-inferiority to surgery)
  - Or established TAVR device (if claiming equivalence to predicate)
- **Randomization:** 1:1 ratio, stratified by center and key risk factors
- **Blinding:** Outcomes assessment blinded (proceduralists cannot be blinded)
- **Primary endpoint:** Composite of all-cause mortality and disabling stroke at 12 months (powered for non-inferiority)
- **Secondary endpoints:**
  - Cardiovascular mortality at 30 days and 1 year
  - Major vascular complications
  - Paravalvular leak rates
  - Device success rate
- **Duration:** Minimum 2 years of follow-up with annual assessments.
- **Analysis:**
  - Intention-to-treat primary analysis
  - Per-protocol sensitivity analysis
  - Pre-specified subgroup analyses
  - Kaplan-Meier survival curves
- **Sample size:** Powered to detect non-inferiority margin (typically 5-7.5% for mortality endpoints).

> Every study should have a statistical analysis plan that sets out which populations will be analysed, which statistical methods will be used and how confounding will be addressed.

### Expected Content: Benefit-risk Assessment

Performance and safety benchmarks identified through the SOTA review will inform the risk-benefit assessment. The clinical evaluation takes into account identified and residual risks, and feed any new findings back into the risk management file.

In some circumstances, the benefit may be determined by predefined clinical success thresholds or acceptance criteria, reducing HbA1c to below 42mmol/mol for example. However, in many cases there is not a clearly defined threshold, placing greater emphasis on findings from a literature review.

Establishing clinical risk is often more challenging than establishing the clinical benefit. In the case of a diagnostic test for melanoma, an important risk to consider is the false positive rate for visual inspection. False positives will have both psychological and economic implications since these patients will have to be referred for further investigation, likely involving consultation with a dermatologist and a biopsy.

The impact of a false positive needs to be weighed against the diagnostic value of the test to determine whether the risk-benefit ratio is acceptable.
