# Device Label

Create a device label that captures key information to promote safe use of your technology.

The device label provides essential written and graphical information that appears on the device or its packaging. It informs users about the identity, regulatory status, usage, and handling of the device.

Much of the information required here will have been generated for other documents, such as the *Device description* and *Instructions for use*, making this one of the easier components of the technical file.

## Minimum Requirements

A device label should feature the following information:

- **Device information** - Include device name, version number, unique device identifier (UDI), manufacturer details and responsible organisation if you do not have a registered business in the jurisdiction where you are applying for certification.
- **Clinical investigation** - Indicate whether the device is intended exclusively for clinical investigation and whether this is stated on the label. For EU Class III devices only, provide details of the latest approved SSCP.
- **Operating instructions** - Input any operating instructions that are printed directly on the device label (e.g. basic usage directions, activation steps).
- **Warnings and precautions** - Provide any warnings that appear on the label, informing users of potential risks. Provide any precautions printed on the label, highlighting steps users must take to ensure safe use and prevent misuse.
- **Instructions for use** - Confirm whether the instructions for use (IFU) will be made available in electronic form only.
- **Special handling, storage and expiration** - Describe any specific conditions for transport, storage, or use (e.g. temperature range, humidity). If applicable, provide the expiration of use-by date of the device.
- **Summary of safety and clinical performance** - Provide a summary of the safety and clinical performance of the device.
- **Expiration date** - Provide the expiration date of the device.

## Common Pitfalls

Thankfully, there isn't a great deal to do here. The main pitfall with the label is **inconsistencies with other documentation** - a pet peeve of any assessor.

> **Warning:** Having a systematic approach to documentation may help to avoid this. The Intended purpose is always a good place to start as this informs key components of the technical file.
>
> The Device description follows nicely and will feature the intended purpose statement. It may be a good idea to complete the Instructions for use and Device label after having crafted the Clinical evaluation report since the IFU will contain safety and performance data.

## Desired Format

We are not too prescriptive about how things are presented, we love technical documentation in all its guises! However, adhering to these tips would delight us:

- Provide the information in text files.
- Ensure each expected device information item is clearly identifiable and searchable.
- Ensure consistency across documentation - key Device information elements (e.g. intended purpose statement) will feature in multiple places throughout the file.
